§1

Organ & Tissue Donation

An organ is a self-contained group of tissues that performs a specific vital function (e.g., heart, kidneys, liver, lungs, pancreas). Tissues are collections of similar cells (e.g., corneas, skin, bone, heart valves) that can be stored in tissue banks and transplanted independently.
Any person, regardless of age, caste, religion, or gender, can pledge to be a donor. Medical suitability is determined at the time of death by authorized medical professionals, per THOTA guidelines.
There is no absolute age limit. For minors under 18, legal guardian consent is mandatory. Corneas can be donated by individuals up to 80–100 years. The clinical team evaluates viability case-by-case at death.
Yes. Having diabetes, hypertension, or other chronic conditions does not automatically disqualify donation. The transplant team assesses each organ individually at the time of death. Active cancer or untreated systemic infections are typically contraindications.
A pledge card is a declaration of your wish to donate organs after death. In India, it is NOT legally binding — the final consent must come from the next-of-kin at the time of death. However, it serves as documented evidence of intent.
No. Organ retrieval is performed by qualified surgeons with the same precision as any other surgery. The body is treated with utmost respect and is returned to the family in a dignified condition suitable for last rites.
No. All costs related to the organ retrieval procedure are borne by the recipient's hospital or the government programme. The donor's family is never charged any fees.
A single deceased multi-organ donor can save up to 8 lives through organ donation (2 kidneys, liver, heart, 2 lungs, pancreas, small bowel) and improve up to 50+ lives through tissue donation (corneas, skin, bone, heart valves).
All major religions in India — Hinduism, Islam, Christianity, Sikhism, Buddhism, and Jainism — support organ donation as an act of compassion and charity. No religion prohibits the saving of human life through donation.
Visit the official NOTTO website (notto.gov.in), fill out the online pledge form with your Aadhaar/ABHA ID, and download your digital pledge certificate. OrganChain additionally secures your intent on-chain for immutable verification.
§2

Organs & Tissues Transplant

NOTTO's allocation algorithm evaluates: (1) blood type compatibility, (2) HLA tissue matching, (3) medical urgency (MELD/PELD scores for liver), (4) time on the waitlist, and (5) geographic proximity to the donor hospital. No socioeconomic factors are considered.
The national waitlist is maintained by NOTTO under the Directorate General of Health Services, Ministry of Health & Family Welfare, GOI. Regional organ-sharing networks (ROTTO/SOTTO) manage state-level lists that feed into the national registry.
Cross-matching is a laboratory test performed between donor and recipient blood/tissue samples to detect pre-formed antibodies. A positive crossmatch indicates incompatibility and the transplant cannot proceed, as it would lead to hyperacute rejection.
Cold ischemia time is the period an organ spends outside the body in cold storage. Benchmarks: Heart 4–6 hrs, Lungs 6–8 hrs, Liver 12 hrs, Kidney 24–36 hrs. Exceeding these windows significantly increases graft failure risk.
Recipients undergo comprehensive evaluation: blood typing, HLA panel reactive antibody testing, cardiac evaluation, pulmonary function tests, psychological assessment, and infectious disease screening (HIV, HBV, HCV). An Authorization Committee reviews each case.
Immunosuppressants prevent the recipient's immune system from attacking the transplanted organ. They must be taken for life. Common regimens include tacrolimus, mycophenolate, and prednisone. Non-compliance is the leading cause of late graft loss.
A Green Corridor is a special traffic route cleared by police between the donor hospital and recipient hospital to minimize organ transport time. Indian cities like Chennai, Mumbai, and Delhi have executed Green Corridors covering 15–30 km in under 10 minutes.
Yes. NOTTO facilitates inter-state organ sharing. If no suitable recipient is found within the state (via SOTTO/ROTTO), the organ is offered nationally. Commercial airlines provide priority boarding for organ transport boxes under existing GOI circulars.
Kidney transplants have a 1-year graft survival rate of ~95%. Liver transplants ~85–90%. Heart transplants ~80–85%. Indian transplant centres (like AIIMS, Global Hospital) achieve outcomes comparable to Western benchmarks.
Tissue banks store donated human tissues (corneas, bone, skin, heart valves) and distribute them to hospitals. They must be registered with NOTTO and follow Standard Operating Procedures for procurement, processing, storage, and quality assurance.
§3

Living Donor Related Transplant

Under THOTA, "near relative" means spouse, son, daughter, father, mother, brother, sister, grandfather, grandmother, grandson, or granddaughter. Donations from near relatives require only hospital-level Authorization Committee approval.
When a willing living donor is biologically incompatible with their intended recipient, they can exchange with another incompatible pair. Donor A gives to Recipient B, and Donor B gives to Recipient A. This is legal and encouraged under THOTA amendments.
Yes, but it requires approval from the state-level Authorization Committee, which conducts thorough scrutiny to ensure no commercial transaction is involved. The donor must demonstrate reasons of "affection or attachment" or pure altruism.
A living donor must be at least 18 years of age and of sound mind to give legally valid consent. There is no upper age cap, but comprehensive medical evaluation must confirm the donor can safely undergo surgery.
Tests include: blood typing, cross-matching, GFR (kidney function), CT angiography (renal vasculature), cardiac evaluation, infectious disease panel (HIV, HBV, HCV, TB), liver function tests, and psychological evaluation.
Yes. The liver regenerates. A living donor can donate 50–60% of their liver, which will regenerate to near-full size in both donor and recipient within 6–8 weeks. This is known as Living Donor Liver Transplant (LDLT) and is widely practiced in India.
Most laparoscopic donor nephrectomy patients are discharged in 3–5 days and can return to normal activities in 4–6 weeks. The remaining kidney compensates fully, and donors can live a normal, healthy life with one kidney.
The Authorization Committee (mandated under THOTA) reviews all living donor transplants, especially non-related ones. It interviews donor and recipient separately, verifies documents, and ensures no coercion or commercial dealing is involved.
Yes. Advanced desensitization protocols (plasmapheresis, rituximab) allow ABO-incompatible kidney transplants at select centres in India. While outcomes are comparable, the procedure requires additional pre-conditioning and careful monitoring.
Studies show living kidney donors have an overall life expectancy comparable to the general population. There is a marginal increase in risk of end-stage renal disease (0.04% vs 0.03%), but with proper follow-up and healthy lifestyle, long-term outcomes are excellent.
§4

Deceased Donor Related Transplant

Brain-stem death is the irreversible cessation of all brain-stem functions. The patient cannot breathe independently and has no cranial nerve reflexes. Under THOTA, brain-stem death is legally recognized as the death of the individual.
A panel of 4 doctors (the treating physician, an independent specialist, a neurologist/neurosurgeon, and a government-nominated doctor) must conduct two sets of tests 6 hours apart. All four must unanimously certify brain-stem death.
Yes. In India, a pledge is an expression of intent, not a legally binding directive. The next-of-kin must provide written consent before organ retrieval can proceed. This makes family awareness and communication crucial.
In brain-stem death, the heart continues beating via ventilator support but all brain functions have ceased. In cardiac death, the heart stops. Brain-stem death donors can donate more organs (heart, lungs, liver, kidneys, pancreas). Cardiac death donors can donate tissues (corneas, skin, bone).
Transplant Coordinators are trained professionals mandated in every transplanting hospital. They counsel grieving families, coordinate brain-stem death certification, liaise with NOTTO/ROTTO for allocation, manage logistics, and ensure legal compliance throughout the donation process.
Once family consent is obtained, hemodynamic maintenance continues while recipients are identified and cross-matched. Retrieval surgery is coordinated across multiple surgical teams simultaneously. The process typically takes 8–12 hours from consent to retrieval completion.
DCD is not yet widely practiced in India. THOTA primarily recognizes brain-stem death for deceased organ donation. However, tissues (corneas within 6 hours, skin, bone) can be retrieved after cardiac death without brain-stem death certification.
The body is surgically closed with full dignity and returned to the family for last rites. The hospital bears all costs related to the retrieval. No visible disfigurement occurs, and an open-casket funeral is possible.
No. Complete anonymity is maintained between the donor family and the recipient. This is a strict protocol under THOTA and NOTTO to prevent any financial or emotional coercion. Basic outcome updates (e.g., "transplant successful") may be shared.
India's cadaveric donation rate is approximately 0.8 per million population (pmp), compared to Spain's ~48 pmp. Tamil Nadu leads (~1.8 pmp) due to robust infrastructure and awareness. The national target is to reach 5 pmp by 2030 through increased awareness and hospital capacity building.
§5

Legal & Ethical Issues

The Transplantation of Human Organs and Tissues Act (THOTA) was enacted in 1994 and amended in 2011 and 2014. It provides the legal framework for organ and tissue donation and transplantation in India, covering both living and deceased donation, and criminalizing organ commercialization.
Absolutely not. THOTA makes organ trafficking a cognizable, non-bailable offence punishable by imprisonment of up to 10 years and a fine of up to ₹1 crore. Any person involved — middlemen, doctors, donors, or recipients — is liable for prosecution.
Under THOTA Section 19, first offence: imprisonment up to 5 years + fine up to ₹20 lakhs. Subsequent offence: imprisonment up to 10 years + fine up to ₹1 crore. Hospitals and doctors can additionally lose their registration and license.
Each state and UT appoints an Appropriate Authority that grants registration to transplant hospitals, enforces THOTA provisions, conducts inspections, investigates complaints of organ commercialization, and has the power to suspend hospital registrations.
No. Performing transplant surgery without registration under THOTA is a criminal offence. Hospitals must meet infrastructure, staffing, and equipment criteria and be inspected by the Appropriate Authority before receiving registration.
Yes. Every allocation decision is cryptographically hashed and recorded on an immutable Ethereum Layer-2 ledger. This creates a 100% auditable trail — no match can be altered or deleted. Any deviation from the algorithm triggers an automated DeviationEvent requiring multi-signature authorization.
Yes, but only from a living near-relative donor. Foreign nationals cannot be placed on the deceased donor waitlist. They must obtain a medical visa, and the Authorization Committee applies additional scrutiny. THOTA prioritizes Indian citizens for deceased donor organs.
Four biomedical ethics principles apply: (1) Autonomy — respecting donor and family wishes, (2) Beneficence — maximizing benefit to the recipient, (3) Non-maleficence — ensuring no harm to the donor, (4) Justice — fair allocation regardless of wealth, caste, religion, or social status.
No. In deceased donation, allocation follows NOTTO's algorithm based on medical criteria only. Directed deceased donation (choosing a specific recipient) is not permitted under THOTA to prevent discrimination and ensure equitable access.
NOTTO monitors transplant activity across all registered hospitals, enforces mandatory reporting of all transplants, audits Authorization Committee decisions, and coordinates with law enforcement to identify and prosecute organ tourism networks operating within India.
